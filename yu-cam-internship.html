Overview

With the changing political climate in the US and in the runup to the 2020 US elections, the news is rife with stories of race-based social unrest, rising protests by supremacist groups, and overt hate speech on-ground and online. We have seen an increase in anti-Semitic tropes, memes, and posts on the web. This is, however, not new.

In 2016, The World Jewish Congress in collaboration with a private company, Vigo Social Intelligence, produced a study identifying and categorizing 382,000 anti-Semitic speech and symbolisms across several social and web channels during 2016. The study suggested that these posts generally fit into five categories or types of anti-Semitism: 1. expressions of hatred against Jews; 2. calls to harm Jews; 3. dehumanization of Jews; 4. Holocaust denial; 5. the use of symbols traditionally associated with anti-Semitism.

What may be new in the last five years are seeming ‘dog whistles’ by politicians and public groups that are legitimizing, if not encouraging, of these behaviors.

Based on the rising intensity of these behaviors and uncharacteristic support by public figures, there is value in comparing the types, sources, and volume of hate speech happening now with that in the past. We propose to identify whether anti-Semitic speech remains the same over the last five years or how it may have changed in the current global climate.

This study contributes to the body of knowledge on hate speech and anti-Semitism by assessing social media reach on various populations, namely the United States. The categories defined in the 2016 report will be reviewed and potentially modified based on recent political and social events as well as social media trends.

It would analyze publicly available social media and potentially integrate that data with other public sources depending on availability and CAM’s interests. The internship should produce analyses.

Analyses may include the following: Fitting current data into the five categories to identify if the categories still hold true today, exploring the relevancy of other categorizations available in the literature or suggested by CAM, performing a keyword analysis to identify emergent categories using large samples of the public data as a representative sample, qualitative assessment of social media trends and the data included in the report, and An assessment in the context of the United States demographic data.

About

Combat Anti-Semitism Movement (CAM)

The Combat Anti-Semitism Movement (CAM) is a non-partisan, global grassroots movement of individuals and organizations, across all religions and faiths, united to combat anti-Semitism. Our unique platform creates partnerships throughout the world, strategically implementing new and creative solutions to combat the world’s oldest hatred. We leverage the collective power of our broad-based coalition to unite individuals of all backgrounds in this fight. CAM is a registered 501(c)(3) entity organized under the Combat Hate Foundation.
